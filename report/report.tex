\begin{abstract}
  In this report based on \cite{sisouk2023wassersteindictionariespersistencediagrams},
  we study the idea of doing non-linear dimensionality reduction on a large dataset of persistence diagrams,
  using atomic persistence diagrams that span the dataset for the hull defined by the optimal transport of
  diagrams.
  We will first give the mathematical intuition on why this could work then provide an algorithm that
  proposes a method to compute such a representation.
	We will finally provide benchmarks on the different methods, implemented in Rust.
	% for a blazingly fast™ result.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
When considering structured datasets mapping to an underlying geometric structure, computing topological
invariants as is done in topological data analysis will lead to the study of persistence diagrams,
large sets of points of the plane mapping to the stability and impactfullness of topological features
when considering more and more of the points in the dataset.
This yields an accurate mapping of the topological homology structure\footnote{We will always consider
	homology on $\mathbb{F}_{2}$ for simplicity.} generated by a meshing of the dataset and thus by the
dataset when the meshing is accurate enough.

Persistence diagrams are thus fundamental tools to understand the geometric structure of data, and have
many practical applications, from clustering (ToMaTo algorithm by Chazal et al.) to non-rigid shape
segmentation. % TODO: add citations
However, such objects live in a highly non-linear space and might have prohibitive quantities of points
to run machine learning algorithms on.

\cite{sisouk2023wassersteindictionariespersistencediagrams} suggests using optimal transport to define
atomic diagrams that allows to represent specific persistence diagrams as barycenters for the Wassertein
geodesics as a way to do non-linear dimensionality reduction over some set of base diagrams that we want to
study.


\section{Mathematical preliminaries}
In this section we will lay out the different mathematical objects we will use when
presenting the algorithm that allows us to optimize our atomic dictionary.

We are given a set $X = \left\{X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}\right\}$ of $N$ persistence diagrams.
Each input diagram is given as the set of its persistence pairs mapped to points in the $2$-dimensional
plane\footnote{Or half-plane above the line $y = x$.}.
In this sense, each diagram $X_{i}$ is given as a finite set in
$\mathbb{R}^{2}$.

We will also consider a cost function between the points of the diagrams by
considering the squared $\ell^{2}$-norm on $\mathbb{R}^{2}$.
This will be the main comparison point between two persistent features.

\paragraph{\texorpdfstring{$2$-dimensional}{2-dimensional} optimal transport}
Optimal transport is the mathematical field concerned with mapping
(dis-)similarities from a space with some sense of geometry to the space of
probability distributions on that space.
More precisely, given two measures $\alpha, \beta$ on spaces $\mathcal{X}$ and
$\mathcal{Y}$, as well as a cost function $c: \X \times \Y \to \R$, it aims at
computing the optimal transport plan $T_{\sharp}\alpha = \beta$ ,
\begin{equation*}
	M = \inf_{T_{\sharp}\alpha = \beta} \int c(x, T(x))\mathrm{d}\alpha(x)
\end{equation*}
where $T_{\sharp}\alpha (B) = \alpha\left(T^{-1}\left(B\right)\right)$ for
$T: \X \to \Y$ that maps points to $\X$ to points in $\Y$ minimising the transport
cost and such that density of $\alpha$ is correctly taken to density in $\beta$.

\smallskip

When $\X = \Y$ and $c$ is some power $p$ of a metric on $\X$, then the optimal transport
cost $M(\alpha, \beta)$ becomes a metric $W_{p}$ on the space $\mathcal{P}(\X)$ of
probability measures on $\X$.
This gives us a notion of geodesics on $\mathcal{P}(\X)$ and as such a notion
of barycenters as minimisers of the Fréchet energy
\begin{equation*}
	E_{F}\left(X, \left(X_{i}\right), \left(\lambda_{i}\right)\right) =
	\sum_{i = 1}^{n}\lambda_{i}W_{p}^{p}(X, X_{i}),
\end{equation*}
where $\left(\lambda_{1}, \ldots, \lambda_{n}\right) \in \Delta_{n}$ the probability simplex
of size $n$.
In the case studied here, we will take the $\ell^{2}$ norm and thus consider $p= 2$
in the above equation.

\smallskip

One can see a persistence diagram $X_{i}$ as a uniform probability distribution over
its points in $\R^{2}$.
However, in the above definition, one cannot \emph{split mass} present on one point,
meaning that if $\abs{X_{i}} \not\mid \abs{X_{j}}$, there will not be any transport
plan between $X_{i}$ and $X_{j}$.
To remove that issue, we will provide a notion of augmentation on diagrams.
Notice that for persistence diagrams, points $(b, d) \in \R^{2}$ where $b = d$ do
not have any real topological feature associated.
As such, we will augment the set of points in $X_{i}$ by adding to it the projections
on the diagonal of $\R^{2}$ of each point in each of the $(X_{j})_{j \neq i}$.

This will make all diagrams have the same number of points. We then simply need to
extend our cost function to be zero between two points in the diagonal (again,
this comes from the fact that a point on the diagonal can be seen, as any other
point on the diagonal, as a \emph{fake} topological feature) and we can compute
transport plans in this setting, with a cost function that is convex.

\paragraph{Matchings for transport plans}
Computing efficiently the transport plan between two diagrams will be crucial when evaluating barycenters.
Indeed, as we will see in the next section, we aim run a gradient descent on atoms and weights computing
barycenters of the set of base diagrams.
To do so, we simply consider the problem of computing an optimal assignation from diagram $X_{i}$ to
diagram $X_{j}$ as a matching problem.
Indeed, since $\abs{X_{i}} = \abs{X_{j}}$ after augmentation, a transport plan from the uniform
distribution on $X_{i}$ to the one on $X_{j}$ is purely an assignation on the points of $X_{i}$ to the
points of $X_{j}$ that minimizes the cost.

This problem is formally modeled when considering the complete bipartite graph $X_{i} \sqcup X_{j}$ with
the cost matrix associated to the distance defined earlier.

\section{Optimization of atoms}
In this section we will describe the procedure used to compute the atoms $\mathcal{D} = \left\{a_{1}, \ldots, a_{m}\right\}$ and
weights $\Lambda = \{\llambda_{1}, \ldots, \llambda_{N}\}$ for the Wasserstein barycenter description of our
dataset $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{N}$.

\subsection{General principle}
The algorithm works as a joint optimization on two sub-problems: the optimization on the atoms $\D$ and the
optimization on the vector of barycentric weights for $X_{i}$ denoted $\llambda_{i} \in \Delta_{m}$
in order to minimize the sum of the Fréchet energies (the \emph{dictionary energy})
\begin{equation*}
	E_{D}(\Lambda, \D) = \sum_{n = 1}^{N}W_{2}^{2}(Y(\llambda_{n}, \D), X_{n}),
\end{equation*}
where $Y(\llambda_{n}, \D) = \underset{Y}{\rm argmin} E_{F}(Y, \D, \llambda_{n})$.

It will thus work in two gradient descent steps:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item The weight optimization for a fixed $\D$;
	\item The dictionary optimization for a fixed $\Lambda$.
\end{enumerate}
We refer the reader to Section 3 of \cite{sisouk2023wassersteindictionariespersistencediagrams}
for the detail of the computations, as those are not the focus of this report.

\paragraph{Weight optimization}
Since the dictionary energy for the weights is separated, we consider here only a single $X_{i}$ to give
the expression of the gradient and the descent step.
Since our optimal transport plans are optimal assignments $\phi_{1}, \ldots, \phi_{m}$ between
$Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)$ and the $k$-th atom $a_{k}$, we can write the energy as
\begin{equation*}
	E_{W}(\llambda_{i}) = W_{2}^{2}(Y(\llambda_{i}, \D), X_{i}) = \sum_{j = 1}^{\abs{Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)}}\norm{\sum_{k = 1}^{m}
		\llambda_{i, k}\left(a_{k}^{\phi_{k}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)}\right)}^{2}
\end{equation*}
where $\psi$ is the optimal assignment between $X_{i}$ and its approximation $Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)$, and $d^{\phi(j)}$ is the $\phi(j)$-th not-diagonal point of diagram $d$.
Finally, we find the gradient of the energy
\begin{equation*}
	\nabla E_{W}(\llambda_{i}) = 2\sum_{k = 1}^{m}\sum_{j = 1}^{\abs{Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)}}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\left(a_{1}^{\phi_{1}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)}\right)^{T} \\
		\vdots                                             \\

		\left(a_{m}^{\phi_{m}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)}\right)^{T}
	\end{bmatrix}
	\left(\llambda_{i,k}\left(a_{k}^{\phi_{k}\left(j\right)} - x^{\psi\left(j\right)}\right)\right)
\end{equation*}
and we compute the barycentric weights at the next iteration by one step of gradient descent of step
$\rho_{\llambda_{i}}$ which converges when $\rho_{\llambda_{i}}$ is less than one over the lipschitz
constant of $\nabla E_{W}$.

\paragraph{Atom optimization}
The dictionary energy being not separated on the atoms but on the dataset, we again consider only one input
diagram and repeat the process.
Again we have the energy associated to $X_{i}$ being
\begin{equation*}
	E_{A}(\D) = W_{2}^{2}(Y(\llambda_{i}, \D), X_{i}) = \sum_{j = 1}^{\abs{Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)}}\norm{\sum_{k = 1}^{m}
		\llambda_{i, k}\left(a_{k}^{\phi_{k}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)}\right)}^{2}
\end{equation*}
and we can thus compute the pointwise atom energy associated to the atom points $\D^{j}$
matching to a given point $y^{j}$ of the barycentric approximation $Y(\llambda_{i}, \D)$ of $X_{i}$ by
\begin{equation*}
	e_{A}(\D^{j}) = \norm{\sum_{k = 1}^{m}\llambda_{i, k}(a_{k}^{\phi_{k}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)})}^{2}:weights
\end{equation*}
and thus computing it's gradient
\begin{equation*}
	\nabla e_{A}(\D^{J}) = 2
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\llambda_{j, 1} & \cdots & \llambda_{j, m}
	\end{bmatrix}^{T}
	\left(\sum_{k = 1}^{m}\llambda_{j, k}\left(a_{k}^{\phi_{k}(j)} - x^{\psi(j)}\right)^{T}\right).
\end{equation*}
Finally, we simply iterate a gradient descent with a step $\rho_{\D^{j}}$ which converges when $\rho_{\D^{j}}$ is less than one over the lipschitz constant of $\nabla e_{A}$.

\subsection{Implementation and optimizations}
The algorithm above was implemented in Rust to try and reproduce the results of the paper.
We chose to represent the diagrams as dynamic arrays of points, and implemented as many optimizations as
we could.

\paragraph{Barycenter algorithm}
In \cite{Vidal_2019}, on which \cite{sisouk2023wassersteindictionariespersistencediagrams} is partially
based, the authors present a few optimizations for what they call a \emph{progressive} approximation of
barycenter for the Wasserstein metric of persistence diagrams.

\begin{algorithm}
	\caption{Barycentric Approximation Algorithm}\label{alg:bary}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\Function{Barycenter}{$\D = (a_{i}, \ldots, a_{m}), \llambda$}
		\Output{: Barycenter $Y(\llambda, \D)$}
		\EndOutput
		\State $Y(\llambda, \D) \gets a_{i}$ \Comment{$i$ randomly chosen in $[1, m]$}
		\While{$E_F$ decreases and $t \leq t_{\rm max}$}\Comment{Do at most $t_{\rm max}$ iterations}
		\For{$i \in [1, m]$}
		\State{$\phi_{i} \gets \mathrm{Matching}_{\delta}((a_{i})_{\rho}, Y(\llambda, \D))$}
		\EndFor
		\State{$Y(\llambda, \D) \gets \mathrm{Update}(\phi_{1}, \ldots, \phi_{m})$}\Comment{Arithmetic mean}
		\State{$\delta \gets \frac{\delta}{5}$}
		\State{$\rho \gets PS(\rho)$}
		\EndWhile
		\Return{$Y(\llambda, \D)$}
		\EndFunction
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

They introduce a few main optimizations, not all of which we implemented.
\begin{itemize}
	\item First, they suggest that the different matching computations retain the previous assignations in
	      memory when creating a matching (see below).
	\item Moreover, when computing the assignments, progressively increasing the accuracy of the algorithm
	      we use while retaining the previous prices for our initialisation yields better and better result
	      with a good performance for the computation time.
	\item They suggest that inside actual persistence diagrams present many artifacts introduced by the
	      filtration used to generate them.
	      Those artifacts come in the form of persistence pairs with small difference between their birth
	      and their death.
	      By beginning the optimization process on a diagram where those artifacts are removed, then
	      progressively adding them back by lowering the threshold of "too low persistence", they are able
	      to locally limit the number of close initialized points and thus distribute among the inputs
	      what the new barycenter points should be.
	      To do so, they define for $\rho > 0$ a threshold, the filtered diagram $a_{\mid \rho}$ consisting of
	      the points $a \in a_{i}$ such that $y_{a} - x_{a} > \rho$.
	      They chose to reduce $\rho$ so that the size of $a_{\mid \rho}$ does not increase by more than
	      $10\%$ at each iteration.
	\item Finally, the authors have added parallelism to their algorithm, which comes at the matching
	      step, which clearly is dependent only on the atom considered and not the others.
	      We did not implement parallelism due to time constraints. % FIXME:
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Matching algorithm}
The main point of optimization is inside the matching computation. Indeed, after each optimization step
we need to recompute every barycentric approximation of our points.
As such, the classical Kuhn-Munkres (or Hungarian) algorithm will not be sufficient.

Following \cite{kerber2016geometryhelpscomparepersistence}, we implemented a variant
of the auction algorithm which makes use of $k$-d trees, a space partioning data-structure which allows for
fast closest-neighbour queries and returns a $1 + \delta$ approximation of the result in a bit faster time.

\smallskip

This comes at the cost of a large overhead computation, but this can be reduced a bit by prize memorization.
In this case, this amounts to initializing the prices for the bidders to the ones they had at the last round
of auction.
The progressive increase in accuracy is done by dividing $\delta$ by $5$ after every major step.

\paragraph{Gradient descent algorithm}
The gradient descent algorithm asks that we compute the barycentric approximations for each base diagram in the data set after each substep of the descent.

However, computing the matchings between atoms and computing the barycenters of the atoms is easily done
in parallel.
Indeed, once the atomic dictionary $\D$ is known, the problem is purely dependent on the weights and thus it
can be solved independently for each $X_{i}$.
Then, the computation of the gradients can also be done in parallel, independently for each $X_{i}$.
